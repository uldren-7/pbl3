1. Preface

This page describes the requirements and system design for a decision-support system aimed at optimizing urban green space placement to reduce Urban Heat Island (UHI) effects and electrical consumption. The document is intended for academic evaluation and project development purposes. It outlines the problem domain, system functionality, data requirements, constraints, and future system evolution.

2. Introduction

The increase in urban environments has created thermal zones which continuously trap heat during the day. This problem can be attributed to low albedo materials and the geometric arrangements urban infrastructures take, creating Urban Heat Islands (UHIs) and the corresponding electrical demand for additional cooling.

Currently, urban centers are experiencing temperature differences of up to 7 degrees Celsius compared to surrounding rural areas. This creates a feedback loop where rising air temperatures increase the demand for electrical air conditioning, which in turn releases more heat into the streets and further intensifies urban warming.

While urban green spaces such as parks and green roofs are proven to mitigate UHI effects through shade and evapotranspiration, their efficiency is limited by competing land usage, sub-optimal placement that fails to target thermal hotspots, and resource constraints such as maintenance requirements and budget limitations.

The primary goal of this project is to maximize thermal energy reduction through optimized green space development. The system maps Urban Heat Islands, models the constraint satisfaction problem (CSP) under real-world constraints, optimizes for maximum reduction of electrical demand, and quantifies the estimated electrical energy savings per household and per region.

4. User Requirements Definition

Overview

The system provides mapping and identification of Urban Heat Islands and models how green developments affect urban temperature distribution. It creates a spatial and temporal simulation where green development locations are subjected to constraints such as land availability and budget.

The system optimizes green space placement to reduce thermal energy and electrical demand while estimating energy savings per household and region.

Functional Requirements

A. Data Input & Processing

ID Requirement
FR-01 The system shall automatically ingest satellite imagery (including Landsat 8/9 thermal infrared bands) to generate an initial grid representation of the urban area.
FR-02 The system shall automatically read and process urban heat data, building footprint data, and water network data without requiring manual file conversion by the user.
FR-03 The system shall treat water network availability as a hard constraint, automatically excluding grid cells where water access is insufficient to support vegetation.
FR-04 The system shall accept population density data as a weighted input that influences the priority scoring of candidate green space locations.
FR-05 The system shall accept population vulnerability indices as a weighted criterion within the placement evaluation framework.
FR-06 The system shall be able to ingest publicly available civic data (e.g. 311 service request data) as a proxy indicator for community need and underservice.
FR-07 The system shall accept parcel valuation and land domain data to inform land acquisition feasibility assessments.
FR-08 The system shall allow users to align placement recommendations with existing capital improvement plans by accepting relevant project data as input.
FR-09 The system shall support a clearly defined input file format and shall provide documentation or tooling to assist users in preparing data in that format.

B. Grid UI & Manual Control

ID Requirement
FR-10 The system shall provide a grid-based user interface that displays the urban area overlaid with the generated green space arrangement.
FR-11 The system shall allow users to manually rearrange green spaces within the grid after automatic generation.
FR-12 The system shall update heat impact visualizations in real time whenever the user manually modifies the arrangement.
FR-13 The system shall allow users to designate specific grid cells as static (immovable), which the optimization model shall treat as hard constraints.
FR-14 The system shall display satellite imagery as the base layer of the grid interface to provide spatial context for manual editing.

C. Constraint System

ID Requirement
FR-15 The system shall allow users to define custom constraints of spatial, linear, and boolean types that are incorporated into the optimization model.
FR-16 The system shall save user-defined constraints and allow them to be reloaded and modified for future projects.
FR-17 The system shall allow users to define separate budget constraints for immediate installation cost and long-term maintenance cost.
FR-18 The system shall allow users to set a maintenance labor capacity limit that restricts the total manageable green space area.
FR-19 The system shall allow users to specify funding source restrictions that limit which placement options are eligible under a given budget.
FR-20 The system shall allow users to define minimum patch size thresholds, below which candidate areas are excluded from consideration.
FR-21 The system shall allow users to toggle between a public-land-only placement mode and a mode that includes potentially acquirable private land.
FR-22 The system shall allow users to define minimum distance buffers from underground utility infrastructure, including deep foundations, subway lines, and water mains.
FR-23 The system shall incorporate right-of-way width data as a spatial constraint that excludes legally restricted corridors from placement.
FR-24 The system shall allow users to define sightline and visibility clearance thresholds that restrict placement in areas requiring unobstructed lines of sight.
FR-25 The system shall flag historic and cultural sites as protected zones where placement is restricted or requires explicit user override.
FR-26 The system shall account for solar enclosure restrictions, ensuring proposed placements do not violate solar access regulations for adjacent structures.
FR-27 The system shall identify street canyon configurations as high-priority intervention zones within the candidate scoring framework.

D. Optimization Model

ID Requirement
FR-28 The system shall implement a weighted multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) framework to evaluate and rank candidate green space locations across all active constraints and objectives.
FR-29 The system shall use land surface temperature reduction as the primary optimization objective.
FR-30 The system shall incorporate park provision rate as a measurable criterion in the MCDA scoring of candidate locations.
FR-31 The system shall incorporate equity of access as a weighted criterion, ensuring underserved or low-income districts are not systematically deprioritized.
FR-32 The system shall allow users to input district equity parameters to guide balanced spatial distribution of green space across administrative areas.
FR-33 The system shall incorporate disaster prevention as a constraint category, flagging placements that may obstruct emergency access routes or evacuation corridors.
FR-34 The system shall be capable of simultaneously incorporating multiple constraint types (hard, weighted, spatial, regulatory) within a single optimization run.

E. Planning Scenarios

ID Requirement
FR-35 The system shall allow users to define named planning scenarios by adjusting parameters including available budget, available land area, and constraint weights.
FR-36 The system shall generate a distinct green space placement recommendation for each planning scenario defined by the user.
FR-37 The system shall allow users to compare the outputs of multiple planning scenarios within the same session.

F. Output & Reporting

ID Requirement
FR-38 The system shall generate a predicted post-intervention land surface temperature map that visually represents estimated temperature distribution after the proposed arrangement is applied.
FR-39 The system shall display the predicted "After" heat map alongside the original "Before" map to enable direct visual comparison.
FR-40 The system shall display temperature legends and distance scales on all generated heat maps.
FR-41 The system shall display a quantitative summary of predicted land surface temperature improvement upon completion of each optimization run.
FR-42 The system shall report estimated energy savings as a secondary metric, clearly labelled as an approximation derived from temperature reduction values.
FR-43 The system shall provide a placement rationale for each suggested green space location, indicating the primary factors driving its selection.
FR-44 The system shall display relevant improvement indices (e.g. estimated temperature reduction, carbon emission reduction proxy) to support reporting and decision-making by city planners.

Non-Functional Requirements

A. Performance

ID Requirement
NFR-01 The system shall be designed to support computation runs of up to multiple days in duration without failure or data loss, prioritizing result accuracy over speed.
NFR-02 The system shall update heat impact visualizations within 3 seconds of any manual modification to the grid arrangement during interactive editing sessions.
NFR-03 The system shall remain responsive for user input during long optimization runs, providing progress feedback at regular intervals.

B. Usability

ID Requirement
NFR-04 The system shall provide a user interface operable by city planners with moderate technical knowledge, without requiring programming or GIS expertise for standard workflows.
NFR-05 The system shall provide inline documentation or tooltips explaining each constraint parameter, metric, and input field to support non-specialist users.
NFR-06 The system shall display clear warnings when a user-defined constraint combination results in no feasible placement options.

C. Reliability & Data Integrity

ID Requirement
NFR-07 The system shall preserve all user-defined constraints, scenario configurations, and session data across application restarts without data loss.
NFR-08 The system shall validate all imported data files for format correctness and completeness before initiating an optimization run, and shall report specific errors to the user.

D. Generalizability & Portability

ID Requirement
NFR-09 The system shall function across different cities by accepting city-specific datasets, constraints, and configurations as inputs, without requiring code-level modification.
NFR-10 The system shall not be restricted to a single pre-trained geographic model; it shall produce valid results for any city for which the required input data and constraints are provided.

E. Scalability

ID Requirement
NFR-11 The system shall support urban area inputs of varying resolution and spatial extent without requiring reconfiguration of the core optimization model.

F. Extensibility

ID Requirement
NFR-12 The system shall allow additional constraint types and environmental metrics to be added without requiring changes to the core optimization framework.

G. Transparency & Auditability

ID Requirement
NFR-13 The system shall log all optimization runs, including input parameters, active constraints, and output metrics, to support auditability and reproducibility.
NFR-14 The system shall clearly distinguish between hard constraints (non-negotiable exclusions) and soft/weighted constraints (preference-based scoring) in all output displays and reports.

Domain Requirements

A. Regulatory & Zoning

ID Requirement
DR-01 The system shall treat land-use zoning classifications (residential, commercial, industrial) as constraint boundaries that define permissible placement areas for each green space.
DR-02 The system shall incorporate building coverage ratio and floor area ratio regulations as spatial constraints that limit the proportion of land available for green space development.
DR-03 The system shall support city-specific mandatory green space ratios and minimum open space requirements as hard constraints in the optimization model.

B. Infrastructure

ID Requirement
DR-04 The system shall restrict green space placement in areas where underground utilities (water pipes, gas lines, electrical cables) would conflict with root growth or excavation requirements.
DR-05 The system shall restrict placement in areas where above-ground structures (power lines, streetlights, traffic signals) impose height or spatial conflicts with proposed typologies.
DR-06 The system shall account for road access requirements and street layout geometries when evaluating roadside or street-level green space placements.

C. Environmental & Ecological

ID Requirement
DR-07 The system shall account for soil quality, drainage capacity, and groundwater conditions as site suitability factors in the placement evaluation.
DR-08 The system shall restrict species-sensitive or ecologically protected areas (e.g. conservation zones, biodiversity protection areas) from standard optimization placement unless explicitly overridden.
DR-09 The system shall incorporate waste heat capture potential as a secondary environmental benefit metric available for inclusion in the optimization output.

D. Water & Stormwater

ID Requirement
DR-10 The system shall consider stormwater management capacity (infiltration, retention, and flood reduction) as an additional benefit metric when evaluating green space placements in low-lying or high-runoff areas.
DR-11 The system shall account for urban drainage and flood prevention regulations when evaluating placement in areas with known runoff pathways or flood risk.

E. Cultural & Heritage

ID Requirement
DR-12 The system shall apply preservation-priority overrides in culturally or historically protected areas, where heritage regulations may restrict or override optimization objectives.
DR-13 The system shall flag areas subject to heritage restrictions with explicit warnings and shall not place green spaces there by default without user confirmation.

F. Safety

ID Requirement
DR-14 The system shall ensure that no proposed green space arrangement obstructs designated evacuation routes or emergency access corridors, treating these as non-negotiable exclusion zones.
DR-15 The system shall account for fire risk factors, ensuring that proposed vegetation density in fire-prone configurations does not exceed safety thresholds defined by the user or regulatory input.

G. Maintenance & Operational Feasibility

ID Requirement
DR-16 The system shall evaluate the long-term operational feasibility of proposed arrangements by incorporating maintenance cost, irrigation demand, and management complexity as planning criteria alongside optimization objectives.
DR-17 The system shall ensure that all recommended green space arrangements are practically sustainable given the user-defined maintenance labor capacity and budget constraints, and shall flag any arrangement that exceeds these limits.

6. System Evolution

6.1 Scope Evolution

Since the current system cannot accurately predict macro-scale environmental changes such as blocking or redirecting large-scale hot air flow using vegetation, future system evolution may extend the scope to include macro-scale urban climate modelling.

Future versions of the system may evaluate how large-scale urban configurations influence airflow and temperature distribution, including factors such as wind tunnel corridors, aerodynamic drag characteristics, Sky View Factor, Bowen Ratio, Frontal Area Index, pollution deposition velocity, and advanced airflow and heat modelling integrations.

The system may also extend support for additional green space typologies such as green roofs, vertical forests, and vertical greenery systems. Future improvements may include modelling the differences in cooling efficiency, maintenance cost, irrigation demand, and water use efficiency between various green space typologies.

6.2 Data Processing Evolution

Current datasets are heavily affected by complex interactions between urban infrastructure and meteorological developments that are difficult to separate accurately. For example, highways may create wind tunnel effects while skyscrapers may block or redirect natural airflow patterns.

Future system evolution may improve data processing methods to better distinguish the effects of urban infrastructure and environmental conditions. This may include integrating additional factors such as sunlight availability, shading patterns, solar radiation exposure, prevailing wind patterns, urban microclimate characteristics, and the influence of proposed green space typologies on local airflow within dense urban street configurations.
